# Good Strategy Bad Strategy

**Author:** Richard Rumelt  
**Purpose:** Evaluate and formulate strategy using the "kernel" framework (Diagnosis, Guiding Policy, Coherent Actions). Real strategy has three parts that form a coherent whole.  
**Use when:** Users need to evaluate whether a strategy is real or fluff, formulate a strategy from a messy situation, critique a strategic plan, write a strategy document, prepare a board deck with strategic framing, or distinguish between goals and actual strategy

**Most strategies are just goals, wishlists, or slogans.** Real strategy has three parts that form a coherent whole:
1. Diagnosis

## ğŸ—ï¸ The Strategy Kernel

### Part 1: Diagnosis

**What's going on here? Clear, honest statement of the challenge.** Simplifies reality by identifying critical factors.

**Probing questions:**
- What is the single most critical challenge?
- What makes this hard?
- What have we been ignoring?
- Which one problem unlocks the rest?

### Part 2: Guiding Policy

**What's our approach? NOT a goal â€” a method.** Channels action by ruling things out. Creates advantage through focus.

**Probing questions:**
- Does this rule things out?
- Does this explain HOW?
- Would a competitor choose differently?
- Does this create focus or please everyone?

### Part 3: Coherent Actions

**What specifically will we do?** Coordinated, feasible actions that reinforce each other. Resource allocation must match.

**Probing questions:**
- Do actions reinforce each other?
- Are they feasible given actual capabilities?
- Do they follow from the guiding policy?
- What must we STOP doing?

**Flag any of these four hallmarks:**

### Fluff
- Buzzword inflation that says nothing
- Failure to face the challenge
- Mistaking goals for strategy â€” Goals without HOW
- Bad strategic objectives â€” Disconnected wishlists, no prioritization

### The Trap: Mistaking Goals for Strategy

| Goal | NOT Strategy |
|---|-----------|
| "Double revenue" | Missing: customer acquisition, pricing, product |
| "Enter new market" | Missing: differentiation, entry strategy |
| "Best-in-class customer service" | Missing: which segments, what trade-offs |

**Good strategic objectives** are SMART and flow from kernel.

### Sources of Power

- Leverage: effort where it has greatest effect
- Proximate objectives: achievable goals that clear the fog
- Chain-link systems: performance limited by weakest link
- Design: tight integration where whole exceeds parts
- Focus: concentrating resources on a narrowed front
- Dynamics: riding waves of change
